Focusing

The research-backed practice that trains unprejudiced presence

What is Focusing?

Focusing is a practice of staying with the "felt sense"—the preverbal bodily knowing of experience—and allowing it to unfold with curiosity. Rather than analyzing with your intellect, you befriend your inner experience with patience.

This way of paying attention is natural for nearly half the population, though mostly unconscious. The step-by-step Focusing method makes this life-forwarding practice accessible at will in any situation.

For homeopathic practice, Focusing offers multiple benefits:

  • Before consultations: It helps practitioners clear personal agendas, biases, and past experiences before beginning a session.
  • During sessions: The undercurrent of Focusing skill keeps you mindful of what inside you might hijack the client's process, allowing you to gently set that aside and remain unprejudiced.
  • For personal growth: It supports the homeopath's lifelong evolution in pushing personal boundaries as a practitioner of the unprejudiced healing art.
"Focusing is a way of paying attention to one's being-in-the-world, one's interaction as it is experienced through the individual (but not separate) body." — Eugene Gendlin

Meet Eugene T. Gendlin

Eugene Gendlin (1926-2017) was an Austrian-born American philosopher and psychologist who developed the practice of Focusing. While researching what made psychotherapy effective at the University of Chicago, he discovered that successful clients shared a common trait: they naturally paused to check in with a bodily felt sense of their issues before putting things into words.

This insight led Gendlin to develop Focusing as a teachable method, making this natural process accessible to everyone. He spent decades refining the technique and training others, founding The International Focusing Institute to share this work globally.

Gendlin authored numerous books, including Focusing (1978) and Experiencing and the Creation of Meaning, bridging philosophy, psychology, and experiential practice. His work emphasizes that the body carries a kind of knowing that transcends verbal thought—a wisdom we can learn to access and trust.

Why Focusing?

Focusing is a research-backed method developed by philosopher Eugene Gendlin for accessing "felt sense"—the pre-verbal, bodily awareness of one's experiencing. For homeopaths, it's the missing link between mechanical questioning and true unprejudiced observation.

"Focusing is a way of paying attention to one's being-in-the-world, one's interaction as it is experienced through the individual (but not separate) body."
— Eugene Gendlin, philosopher and psychotherapist

Dual Function in Homeopathy

1. Therapeutic Process

When patients learn to stay with their felt sense—that vague, unclear bodily knowing—something shifts before any remedy is given. The process itself initiates healing by helping patients connect with what their body already knows.

You become therapeutic regardless of perfect similimum.

2. Diagnostic Clarity

Focusing makes implicit patterns explicit. When a patient says "I feel stuck," Focusing helps them discover: "It's like a heavy weight on my chest, pressing down, making it hard to breathe." This precision guides remedy selection.

Felt sense becomes data AI can work with.

Why Name It Explicitly?

We could call it "empathetic listening" or "deep presence" and leave it vague. Instead, we name Focusing because:

  • Research credibility: Decades of psychotherapy outcome studies validate its effectiveness
  • Systematic training: Distinguishes learnable skills from hoped-for osmosis
  • Philosophical depth: Honors Gendlin's existential phenomenology—the same tradition Hahnemann drew from
  • Professional legitimacy: Connects homeopathy to body-centered psychotherapy and somatic healing
